What we believe

Architecture is a social and environmental act. Our work is guided by three fundamentals: context, craft, and climate. We begin each project by listening — to site conditions, to the people who will inhabit the building, and to the subtle stories contained in the land. This humility leads to architecture that is both elegant and rigorous.

Our process weaves research, thoughtful material selection, energy-conscious strategies, and precise detailing. Whether we're refurbishing a heritage home, designing a community center, or delivering a small urban infill, our goal is always to create spaces that age gracefully and support meaningful experience.

Collaborative design

We work as collaborators. The best outcomes arise when clients, engineers, consultants and communities are part of the conversation from the earliest moments. This collaborative approach reduces risk, enriches design and creates buildings with long-term cultural value.

Material honesty

We select materials for durability, low environmental impact, and tactile quality. The palette favors natural finishes—timber, stone, clay and carefully specified metals—paired with modern engineering where appropriate.

Climate-responsible strategies

Every project integrates passive design tactics, efficient systems, and lifecycle thinking to reduce energy use and carbon impact. We design for comfort first, then add technology thoughtfully and minimally.

Featured project — The River House

The River House

Perched on an angled riverside site, The River House explores indoor-outdoor relationships through layered terraces, operable screens, and long-span timber beams. The house repurposes a ruined boathouse frame, preserving memory while inserting contemporary performance.

Key moves

  • Adaptive reuse of an existing frame, reducing embodied carbon.
  • Thermally-broken glazing and passive-stack ventilation for summer comfort.
  • Locally-sourced timber and stone to anchor the building within its place.

Essays — short reads on craft & practice

1. The Slow Art of Detail

Details are the final sentence in an architectural story. They determine how a threshold feels, how light filters into a room, and how a handrail will weather over decades. Over-investing in detail is not indulgence — it's the path to buildings that sing.

2. Climate as Constraint and Opportunity

Designing for climate is not merely an engineering problem; it is an aesthetic opportunity. Orientation, materiality and thermal mass become compositional elements that shape daylight, shadow and the lived experience of a space.
